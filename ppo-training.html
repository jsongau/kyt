Lesson 01

What is a PPO dental plan?

The model behind almost every individual plan a patient will buy — and why it beats the alternatives for someone who wants to keep seeing us.

PPO stands for Preferred Provider Organization. It's a type of dental insurance built around a network of dentists who have agreed to accept the carrier's negotiated fees. The patient still gets to choose any licensed dentist, but they pay the least when they stay in-network — which is exactly why a PPO is the right answer for a patient who wants to keep coming to KYT.

An individual PPO is simply a PPO a person buys for themselves, rather than getting it through an employer. They enroll directly with the carrier, pay a monthly premium, and the plan activates on a set effective date — sometimes the next business day. That speed is the whole pitch: a patient who lost their job, aged off a parent's plan, or is stuck in an employer waiting period can be covered before their next visit.

The reason PPO beats the two common alternatives comes down to freedom and reach:

The one-line version for patients

"A PPO lets you keep seeing us and still get insurance to pay a share of the work — an HMO would make you switch dentists, and a discount card isn't insurance at all."

Five terms decide everything about a PPO. Learn these and you can read any plan on the shelf: the premium (monthly cost), the deductible (your first dollars before the plan pays), coinsurance (the share the plan covers per tier), the annual maximum (the most it pays per year), and the waiting period (the delay before a tier of work is usable). We unpack each one next.

Lesson 02

How the coverage actually works

Premium, deductible, coinsurance tiers, and the annual maximum — the four numbers that decide a patient's real bill.

Every PPO sorts dental work into three tiers and pays a different share of each. The pattern almost never changes — only the percentages and the fine print do.

100%
Preventive
Cleanings, exams, x-rays. Usually free, day one.
~80%
Basic
Fillings, simple extractions. You pay ~20%.
~50%
Major
Crowns, root canals, dentures, implants. You pay ~half.
Analogy — "split the check"

Coinsurance is just how the bill gets split. On preventive the plan buys the whole meal; on basic you cover the tip; on major you split it down the middle. The cheaper the plan, the more of the major-work check the patient picks up — which is why premium alone never tells the story.

Two more numbers sit on top of those tiers:

The deductible is the patient's first out-of-pocket dollars on restorative work — usually about $50 — before the plan starts reimbursing. Preventive cleanings are almost always exempt, so a patient can use the plan for a free cleaning without ever touching the deductible.

The annual maximum is the ceiling: the most the plan will pay in one plan year. Hit it, and everything after is 100% on the patient — even work the plan would normally cover at 80%. It resets each year. Our plans range from a $1,000 cap (UHC) to $10,000 (MetLife).

Worked example — read this out loud once

A patient needs a $1,200 crown on a plan that covers major at 50% with a $50 deductible and a $1,500 annual max. They pay the $50 deductible, then split the remaining $1,150 → the plan pays ~$575, the patient pays ~$575. Because $575 is well under the $1,500 cap, the plan still has room left for the rest of the year.

One subtlety worth teaching: many cheap plans phase major coverage — they pay 20% in Year 1 and step up to 50% in Year 2. Ameritas is our example. Great premium, but a patient who needs a crown immediately should know they'll only get 20% back this year.

Lesson 03

Waiting periods — the part patients get wrong

A plan can be active and still not pay for the work a patient needs. This is the single most important thing to explain honestly, up front.

A waiting period is the delay between the day coverage starts and the day a tier of treatment becomes usable. Preventive care almost always starts day one. Basic work often waits 0–3 months. Major work — crowns, root canals, implants — commonly waits 6–12 months.

Analogy — "the new-gym rule"

Joining a PPO is like joining a gym. You can use the treadmill the day you sign up (that's preventive). The free-weights room opens a few weeks later (basic). But the personal-trainer-and-sauna package — the expensive stuff — doesn't unlock for months (major). The membership is real on day one; not every room is.

The trap: "active" ≠ "usable"

"Your plan is active — but that doesn't always mean every service is usable yet. Let's confirm which treatments are covered today versus which ones have a waiting period, so there are no surprises when you sit in the chair."

Why do waiting periods exist? They stop people from enrolling, getting a $2,000 crown, and cancelling the next month. That's also the trade-off you can explain: no-wait plans charge a higher premium; long-wait plans are cheaper but lock the patient out of major coverage early. Ameritas charges more precisely because it has no waiting periods; UHC is cheap partly because it doesn't cover major work at all.

The practical move at the front desk: match the timeline to the treatment. A patient who needs a crown next month and a plan with a 12-month major wait is a mismatch you should catch before they enroll — point them to a no-wait or fast-ramp plan instead (Lesson 5).

Lesson 04

The fine print patients deserve to hear

The missing-tooth clause, frequency limits, and the difference between an effective date and a waiting period. Disclose these honestly — every time.

The missing-tooth clause is the one that burns patients most often. Many PPOs will not pay to replace a tooth that was already missing before the policy started. If a patient lost a molar last year and buys a plan today hoping to get an implant covered, the missing-tooth clause can deny it entirely.

Say this verbatim before quoting any implant or bridge

"Because that tooth was already missing before this policy starts, the plan likely won't cover the implant to replace it — that's the missing-tooth clause, and I want you to know it upfront. Let's request a pretreatment estimate in writing before you commit."

Gotcha 02

Frequency limits

Plans cap how often they'll pay: cleanings/exams ~2×/year, bitewing x-rays ~1×/year, a panoramic x-ray every 3–5 years, a crown once per 5–7 years per tooth. Do it early and the patient pays 100%. Always check the last service date.

Gotcha 03

Effective date ≠ waiting period

The effective date is when coverage begins. The waiting period is when a tier becomes usable. A plan can be effective tomorrow yet still make a patient wait 12 months for a crown. Quote both separately.

Two more honest disclosures worth memorizing. Adult orthodontics is rare on individual PPOs — Delta is our one exception, and even then expect lifetime caps. And the annual maximum can run out mid-treatment: a big case can exhaust a $1,000 cap on a single crown, leaving the rest of the year at full price. Flag it before treatment, not after.

The compliance line — we are educators, not sellers

We're an independent practice, not affiliated with any carrier. We share plan facts and help patients find enrollment links — we never tell a patient which plan to buy as advice, interpret their coverage, or guarantee benefits. When a patient asks "which one should I buy?", hand off to a licensed agent and confirm all benefits with the carrier in writing. Collect only the minimum member info needed, and keep it secure (HIPAA).
